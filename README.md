# Savonius Roadside Wind Turbine CAD Model

## Overview

This project contains a simplified SolidWorks CAD model of a Savonius vertical-axis wind turbine designed for small-scale wind power harvesting in roadside environments. The model is based on a physical PVC-style prototype and represents the main mechanical structure of a compact turbine that can capture wind flow generated by natural wind and vehicle-induced airflow.

The turbine uses a vertical-axis Savonius-style rotor, which is suitable for low-speed, turbulent, and changing wind directions. These conditions are common near roads, highways, tunnels, medians, and other transportation corridors where airflow is not always steady or aligned in one direction. The design focuses on simplicity, manufacturability, and clear mechanical representation rather than high aerodynamic optimization.

## Project Motivation

Roads and highways experience continuous airflow from passing vehicles, especially in areas with frequent traffic. This moving air contains usable kinetic energy that can potentially be harvested using small wind turbines placed near the roadside, road dividers, or highway barriers.

This CAD model explores the mechanical structure of a simple vertical-axis turbine that could be used as a conceptual prototype for roadside wind energy harvesting. The goal is to create a clean digital version of the physical prototype for visualization, documentation, future testing, and design improvement.

## Design Purpose

The purpose of this model is to represent the main components of a small Savonius-type wind turbine for roadside energy harvesting applications. The model can be used for engineering documentation, presentation, early-stage design review, and future development of a more complete wind power harvesting system.

The design is intentionally simplified to focus on the core structure:

* A stable base for mounting
* A vertical support pole
* A central rotating hub
* Curved wind-catching blades
* Rounded PVC-like geometry
* A compact layout suitable for small-scale prototype development

## Features

## Main Components

* **Base:** Provides structural support and stability for the turbine.
* **Tapered Connector:** Transitions from the larger base to the vertical support pole.
* **Vertical Support Pole:** Holds the rotor assembly at an elevated position.
* **Top Hub:** Connects the rotating blade structure around the central axis.
* **Curved Savonius Blades:** Capture airflow and generate rotational motion.
* **Filleted Edges:** Improve the visual realism of the PVC-style prototype.

## Roadside Wind Harvesting Concept

The turbine is intended to conceptually harvest wind energy from airflow near roads. In roadside environments, wind can come from multiple directions and can be highly turbulent due to vehicle movement, surrounding structures, and changing weather conditions.

A vertical-axis Savonius rotor is useful in this type of environment because it does not need to be pointed directly into the wind. Unlike horizontal-axis turbines, a Savonius turbine can accept airflow from different directions, making it better suited for compact roadside installations and early-stage prototyping.

Potential roadside applications include:

* Highway medians
* Road dividers
* Tunnel entrances and exits
* Parking areas
* Campus roads
* Low-power lighting or sensor systems
* Small energy harvesting demonstrations

## Current Project Status

The initial simplified CAD model has been completed. The current model includes the main structural geometry of the turbine: the base, tapered connector, central pole, top hub, curved blades, and rounded fillets.

This version was prototyped and assembled using mainly plastic, a small rotor motor, wiring systems, and it was fully tested to generate small amounts of electricity under small wind.

## Engineering Significance

This project demonstrates the use of SolidWorks for early-stage mechanical design and prototype documentation. It connects CAD modeling with a renewable energy application by representing a small vertical-axis wind turbine that could be adapted for roadside wind energy harvesting.

The model is not intended to be a final optimized turbine design. Instead, it serves as a foundation for future mechanical, aerodynamic, and electrical improvements.
